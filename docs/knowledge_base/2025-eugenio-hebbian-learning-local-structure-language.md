## Summary

This paper derives the foundations of an effective human language model inspired by the microscopic constraints of biological learning: learning in the brain is local and unsupervised (Hebbian). The model has two components: (1) a hierarchy of neurons that learns to tokenize words from continuous text—mirroring the process by which readers segment continuous character streams into words—and (2) additional neurons that bind the learned semanticless patterns of the tokenizer into a semanticful token (an embedding). The key architectural principle is that all learning is driven by local Hebbian correlations, without any global error signal or supervision.

The model permits continuous parallel learning without catastrophic forgetting, a persistent challenge in neural network training. The tokenizer performs what the author describes as renormalization group: it exploits the redundancy in language to generate tokens that are always decomposable into a basis set (such as an alphabet), and can mix features learned from multiple languages. This RG-like structure is not an imported analogy but emerges naturally from the Hebbian learning rule operating on the statistical structure of language data. The hierarchical tokenization process effectively coarse-grains the character-level description into word-level and morpheme-level descriptions, analogous to how RG coarse-grains a microscopic theory into an effective macroscopic description.

A striking result is that the structure of the model allows it to learn a natural language morphology without any data—purely from the architectural constraints imposed by the Hebbian learning rule and the hierarchical structure. The language data generated by the model predicts the correct distribution of word-forming patterns observed in real languages, and demonstrates why human speech is microscopically broken up into words. This suggests that the statistical structure of language is not arbitrary but is constrained by the microscopic learning rules that shape it, a perspective that resonates with the RG idea that microscopic symmetries constrain macroscopic behavior.

## Relevance

- **Hebbian learning**: This paper is centrally about Hebbian learning. The entire model is built on the principle that learning is local, unsupervised, and correlation-based—precisely the characteristics of Hebbian plasticity. The paper demonstrates that Hebbian learning alone, without any global error signal, is sufficient to learn complex linguistic structures including tokenization, morphology, and cross-lingual feature mixing. The continuous parallel learning without forgetting is a key advantage of Hebbian rules over backpropagation-based methods.

- **UV-complete physics**: The connection to UV-complete physics comes through the RG-like structure of the tokenization process. The Hebbian tokenizer performs a renormalization group transformation that coarse-grains the character-level (UV) description into word-level (IR) descriptions. The fact that this RG structure emerges naturally from Hebbian learning—rather than being imposed by hand—suggests that local learning rules may inherently generate UV-to-IR flow structures. The decomposability of tokens into a basis set mirrors the concept of UV completion, where the microscopic (UV) theory provides a complete description from which all coarse-grained (IR) observables can be derived. The cross-lingual feature mixing suggests that the RG flow has universality properties, where different UV starting points (different languages) flow to similar IR descriptions.

- **Expressivity**: The paper demonstrates that Hebbian learning, despite being purely local, can produce representations with substantial expressive capacity. The ability to learn morphology without data—purely from architectural constraints—shows that the expressivity of the model is shaped by the learning rule itself, not just the architecture. The hierarchical tokenization structure enables the model to represent compositional structures (words from characters, morphology from words) that would be difficult to capture with a flat, non-hierarchical architecture. This hierarchical expressivity is a direct consequence of the RG-like coarse-graining induced by Hebbian learning.
